\section{Regional growth factor model}%
\label{sec:regional_growth_factor_model}

Modeling the epidemics' spread on a regional level allows us to differentiate between localized and global outbreaks, such as the one in June 2020, highlighted in \Cref{fig:cases_germany}. Additionally, regional level prediction and growth factors are of interest on their own, because \glspl{npi} are enforced on the regional level. Moreover, having access to the spread on the regional level enables, e.g., regression of the growth rate against regional covariates, which in turn sheds light on which factors drive the epidemic.

Instead of modeling the number of cases per day and with delay as we did in \Cref{sec:model_reporting_delay}, we will now model the total number of cases reported within one week for every county in Germany. Here we assume that a sufficient time period has passed, i.e. several days, see \Cref{fig:reporting_delays_cases}, such that the total number of cases is known sufficiently well. This weekly approach has several advantages: first, aggregating over the weekly data gets rid of the weekday effect, at the expense of a lower time resolution. Second, if we are interested in a retrospective analysis, it is sensible to assume all cases have been reported already, so we can avoid modeling the reporting delays. 

However, modeling cases on the regional level comes with its own challenges, as we must account for both the spatial spread and exchange between regions, cf. \Cref{sec:dessiderata}. 

Before we detail our proposed model, let us give an overview over similar approaches to modeling epidemics on a regional level in the literature. 
Each approach is characterized by the type of statistical model used (e.g., for case counts, hospitalizations, or deaths), as well as how spatial data and regional granularity are incorporated.

% methods
For the general approach, we can roughly group the literature into two groups: network-based time series models and classical time series models that account for spatial effects, although there are also hybrid approaches that combine elements from both. 
In the former group, the network structure, represented by a (weighted) graph, is an integral part of the model, whereas in the latter group, spatial dependencies are accounted for by adding spatially correlated random effects, e.g. in spatio-temporal models, rather than an explicit transmission network between regions. 
Our approach falls mostly into the latter category, as we will model correlated growth rates across regions, but also accounts for cross-regional infections through explicit incorporation of data on commuters between regions.

%% network based methods
For the network-based methods, \citep{Armbruster2024Networkbased} propose a generalized network autoregressive model that captures the spatial dependencies between regions through a network structure, outperforming standard ARIMA models that do not account for spatial dependencies. \citep{Fritz2022Combining} combines a graph neural network with a zero-inflated Poisson model to predict cases on the county level in Germany. \citep{Kuhn2021Assessmenta} combine an ODE-based compartmental model with a network-based approach to model cross-county infections, based on commuter data and geo-referenced Twitter data.

%% spatio-temporal methods
The spatio-temporal models account for regional dependencies by adding spatially correlated random effects. \citep{Kline2022Bayesian} construct a Bayesian spatio-temporal model to perform nowcasting for COVID-19 cases in counties in Ohio, USA. They include a spatially correlated random effect to account for dependencies between neighboring counties, similar to our approach presented below. However, we differ from their approach by incorporating commuter data to model dependencies between all regions, not only neighboring ones. \citep{DeNicola2022Regional,Schneble2021Nowcasting} perform Bayesian nowcasting for German counties using splines for temporal and spatial effects. 

Further approaches based on classical time series analysis fit models to individual regions as a pre-processing step to infer epidemiological indicators such as the reproduction number or growth rate, then use spatial models to infer about regional effects. These methods suffer from the major disadvantage that they do not account for spatial dependencies when fitting the initial models, which may lead to biased estimates and underestimated uncertainty. 
\citet{Ives2021Estimating} estimate the reproduction number for counties in the United States individually using a local-level state space model and use a spatial model to smooth these estimates across neighboring counties. 

% gap identified
%% interpretability difficult
However, none of the works presented above provide models that account for spatial uncertainty while remaining interpretable in terms of epidemiologically relevant indicators, such as reproduction numbers or growth factors. 
The model we will develop in this section will aim at filling this gap. 
We will come back to this literature once we have developed a new model to predict \gls{c19} incidences on the regional level, using an \gls{ssm} based approach. 

\subsection{Model}
Similar to the last section, we start by modeling the evolution of cases in time. We now have incidences $I_{t,r}$ reported for reporting date $t$ and region $r$, where there are a total of $R$ regions. For Germany we use the $R=400$ counties as of the Gebietsreform 2021, which united the free city Eisenach with the county of Wartburgkreis \citep{FreistaatThueringen2019Thueringer}. This has several implications for the raw data published by the \gls{rki}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item For data published before 30. June 2021, cases were reported separately for Eisenach and the Wartburgkreis. In pre-processing the data on the county level, we merge these two regions into a single one.
    \item The \gls{rki} reports cases for Berlin's districts separately. Again, we aggregate all of them into a single number of cases for Berlin. 
\end{itemize}

Similar to the last section, we model the evolution of cases by 
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:log-growth-regional-model}
    \log I_{t + 1, r} \approx \log I_{t, r} + \log \rho_{t + 1, r}
\end{align}
where $\rho_{t+1, r}$ is the weekly growth factor in region $r$. Now we deviate from the previous model and set
$$
    \log \rho_{t, r} = \overline{\log \rho}_{t} + u_{t, r},
$$
where $\overline{\log\rho}_{t}$ is the average growth rate and $u_{t,r}$ is the difference between the growth rate in region $r$ and the country wide average. 

For $\overline{\log \rho}_{t}$ we assume, similar to the previous section, a random walk on the log-scale
$$
    \overline{\log\rho}_{t +1} = \overline{\log\rho}_{t} + \varepsilon_{t+1, \overline{\log \rho}},
$$
where $\left(\varepsilon_{t + 1, \overline{\log\rho}}\right)_{t =0, \dots, 1} \iid \mathcal N(0, \sigma^{2}_{\overline{\log\rho}})$.

We will model $u_{t,r}, r = 1, \dots, R$ to be jointly Gaussian, but correlated, which will enable us to model regional dependencies. To motivate our choice for the covariance structure, let us consider how cases are transferred between regions first.

As we are modeling cases on a regional level, we have to account for an exchange of cases as well. To illustrate our approach, suppose that we have for region $r$ $S^{r}$ many secondary cases generated where the primary case belongs to region $r$, but the secondary case may belong to another region $r'$. Here ``{}belonging to''{} signifies that the case is reported in that region, which means that the infectee has registered their center of living to be in this region. Denote by $p_{r,r'}$ the fraction of such cases and set $p_{r,r} = 1 - \sum_{r' \neq r} p_{r,r'}$. 

Under these assumptions, the newly reported cases in region $r$ are 
$$
    \tilde S^{r} = \sum_{r'=1}^{R} p_{r',r} S^{r'} = (P^{T}S)_{r}
$$
for $ P = \left( p_{r, r'} \right)_{r,r' = 1, \dots, R} \in \R^{R\times R}$. As $P \mathbf 1 = \mathbf 1$, $P$ is a stochastic matrix. Assuming now that $S^{r}, r= 1, \dots, R$ are random and i.i.d. with variance $\sigma^{2}_S$, we have 
$$
    \cov \left( \tilde S\right) = \cov \left( P^{T}S, P^{T}S \right) = \sigma^{2}_SP^{T}P.
$$

However, modeling the correlation of newly reported cases turns out to be difficult: the cases will surely be modeled by a Poisson or Negative Binomial distribution, so we would have to decide on a copula to introduce this dependency structure. While this is feasible in principle, we opt for an easier way. Instead of modeling correlated incidences $I_{t + 1, r}$, we model correlated growth rates $\log \rho_{t + 1, r}$, by taking $\cov \left( u_{t} \right)$ to be $\sigma^{2}_SP^{T}P$. By \eqref{eq:log-growth-regional-model}, conditional on $I_{t,r}$, this also captures regional correlation, without having to specify an involved joint distribution for the incidences. Note however, that this is just a convenient modeling choice whose aim is to replicate the covariance structure of the --- somewhat artificial --- case of having an i.i.d. number of secondary cases across all regions.

As elaborated in \Cref{sec:dessiderata}, we want the regional effects $u_{t,r}$ to be both flexible and, in some sense, stable over time. At first glance, this may appear contradictory, but it is not: we want regions to have their own deviation from the mean, e.g., to capture region-specific effects, and these deviations should not change too much over time. At the same time, we want the model to be flexible in adapting to local outbreaks and to allow for fast dynamics when needed. 

We resolve this apparent tension by reframing stability: instead of requiring the deviations to be constant, we interpret stability stochastically, meaning that the marginal distribution of the regional effects should remain the same at every point in time, i.e. we introduce autocorrelation and model $u_{t,r}$ to be a stationary process. Under this assumption, time-localized changes are still possible, but certain properties of the marginal $u_{t,r}$ are still time-invariant.

Thus, it makes sense to model $u_{t}$ as a stationary process in time. The simplest, non-trivial, stationary process is a vector-autoregressive process 
$$
    u_{t + 1} = \alpha u_{t} + \varepsilon_{t + 1, u}
$$
where $\alpha \in (-1, 1)$ and $\iid$ $\varepsilon_{t + 1,u} \sim \mathcal N(0, \Gamma)$, where $\Gamma$ is a positive definite matrix. By the above discussion, we set $\Gamma = (1-\alpha^{2}) \sigma^{2}_{S}P^{T}P$ so that the stationary distribution of $u_{t}, t = 0, \dots, n$  is $\mathcal N(0, \sigma^{2}_SP^{T}P)$. 

To set up our \gls{ssm}, let $X_{t} = \left( \overline{\log \rho_{t + 1}}, u_{t, 1}, \dots, u_{t, R} \right)^{T} \in \R^{R + 1}$. For the observations, we let $Y_{t} = \left( I_{t, 1}, \dots, I_{t, R} \right)^{T}$, the number of cases observed in regions $1, \dots, R$ in the $t$-th week. 

We now must describe how growth factors relate to observed incidences at the regional level. This requires careful consideration of what a regional growth factor represents when there is exchange between regions. We may consider two options: either the growth factor represents the behavior of the region where transmission occurs, or it represents the behavior of the region where the primary case originates. We opt for the first interpretation, as transmission dynamics are strongly influenced by the local environment --- including factors such as local transport, contact patterns in workplaces, and enforced \glspl{npi} --- rather than by intrinsic characteristics of individuals from a given region. Consequently, we first redistribute cases across regions via $P^{T}$, then apply the regional growth factors to these redistributed cases.

Thus, we model the number of cases at time $t + 1$ in region $r$, $I_{t+1, r}$ to follow a negative binomial distribution, conditional on the states $X_{t}$ to be
$$
    I_{t+1,r} | I_{t}, \overline{\log\rho}_{t}, u_{t,r} \sim \nbinom \left( \rho_{t,r} (P^{T}I_t)_{r}, \phi\right),
$$
conditionally independent, where $\phi$ is a globally shared overdispersion parameter (see \Cref{ex:pois_negbinom} for our parametrization of the negative binomial distribution). While the previous observations $I_{t}$ are now conditioned on as well, recall from our discussion at the beginning of \Cref{cha:state_space_models}, that this is not problematic.

To fully specify the model, we have to provide the transfer probabilities $p_{r,r'}$. For these, we use official data by Germany's federal employment agency on commuters. We use two datasets: the number of employees subject to social security contributions (sozialversicherungspflichtige Beschäftigte) per county \citep{BundesagenturfuerArbeitStatistik2021Sozialversicherungspflichtig} and the number of commuters per county  \citep{BundesagenturfuerArbeitStatistik2024Pendleratlas}. We use the number of commuters per county from December 2022, which are no longer directly available on the homepage of the federal employment agency, but can be obtained through the Pendleratlas interface\footnote{See \url{https://statistik.arbeitsagentur.de/DE/Navigation/Statistiken/Interaktive-Statistiken/Pendleratlas/Pendleratlas-Nav.html}.}. In any case, these data are additionally available in the accompanying zenodo repository of this thesis, see \Cref{cha:reproducibility_and_code}. Notice that both datasets are dated after the Gebietsreform in June 2021. Counties are uniquely identified by the Allgemeiner Gemeinde Schlüssel (AGS), a five-digit code, which is also available in the \gls{rki} dataset. 

In this dataset a commuter is someone whose center of life (as registered with the German authorities, \glqq{}Erstwohnsitz\grqq{}) is a different county than the county of their workplace.
For two regions $r,r'$ we let $n_{r}$ be the total number of employees subject to social security contributions per county and $c_{r,r'}$ be the number of commuters from county $r$ to county $r'$, with $c_{r,r} = n_{r} - \sum_{r'\neq r} c_{r,r'}$.
From these data, we calculate $q_{r,r'} = \frac{c_{r,r'}}{n_{r}}$, the fraction of employees subject to social security contributions that have their center of life in region $r$, but are registered to work in region $r'$. 

As this is only a crude approximation to the actual exchange between regions, we let
$$
    p_{r,r'} \propto \bar q + (1- \bar q) \frac{q_{r,r'}}{\sum_{r'' \neq r} q_{r,r''} + C q_{r,r}} \phantom{......} r' \neq r
$$
where we interpret $\bar q$ as a constant socket of exchange between regions and $C \geq 1$ as an additional proportion of stay at home inhabitants that are not captured by $q_{r,r}$, e.g. elderly or children. The proportionality is owed to the restriction $\sum_{r' = 1}^R p_{r,r'} = 1$. Let us highlight some instances of $P$.  

If $\bar q = 0$ and $C = 1$, then $p_{r,r'} = q_{r,r'}$, which would distribute secondary cases according to the commuting given by the official data from Germany's federal employment agency. 

If $\bar q = 1$ or $\bar q \to 1$ with fixed $C$, $P = \frac{1}{R}J\in\R^{R\times R}$, where $J$ is the matrix with all entries equal to $1$. We can interpret this as the region a secondary case is reported in being chosen uniformly at random across all regions, regardless of the original region of the primary case. 

If $\bar q \in [0,1)$ and $C \to \infty$, then $P \to I\in\R^{R \times R}$, where $I$ is to identity matrix. In this setting all regions evolve independently of one another.

Thus, the model is flexible enough to account for these practically relevant scenarios, and interpolate between them.

Our final model is parameterized by
$$
    \theta = \left( \log \sigma^{2}_S, \operatorname{logit} \alpha, \log (C - 1), \logit \bar q, \log \sigma^{2}_{\overline{\log \rho}}, \log \phi\right),
$$
where we have reparameterized to an unconstrained $\theta \in \R^{6}$. The model has a linear signal 
$$
    S_{t} = \left(\overline{\log \rho_{t}} + u_{t,r}\right)_{r = 1, \dots, R},
$$
which makes inference fast, as the approximating \gls{glssm} in the \gls{la} and \gls{eis} method only requires $\mathcal O(n\,R)$ many parameters. Again, we use \gls{mle} to determine $\theta$, using the methods from \Cref{sec:maximum_likelihood_estimation}. As the models state dimension is quite large, we have occasionally encountered numerical difficulties when using \acrshort{eis}, especially resulting in negative variances leading to invalid proposals. For this reason, we resort to using the more stable \gls{la} for all procedures in this chapter.


We aggregate the reported cases from the \gls{rki} on a weekly level. We orient ourselves at the ECDC's ForecastHub, whose guidelines read
\begin{quote}
    Forecast horizons should use the Epidemiological Week (EW) format, defined by the US CDC. Each week starts on Sunday and ends on Saturday.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/european-modelling-hubs/covid19-forecast-hub-europe/wiki/targets-and-horizons}}
\end{quote}
Thus, the observations $I_{t, r}$ consist of the number of reported cases in region $r$ that were reported from the $t$-th Sunday until the $t$-the Saturday in the period of interest and to simplify matters, we identify the index $t$ with the $t$-th Saturday in the period under consideration. 

\subsection{Results}
We will demonstrate the usefulness of our new model by applying it to two scenarios. First, recalling the local outbreak highlighted in \Cref{fig:cases_germany}, occurring in the middle of June 2022 \citep{Gunther2020SARSCoV2}, we show that using our model gives reasonable estimates of growth rates when incidences are low, while allowing for local outbreaks. Our model can then be used to give sensible forecasts, outperforming baseline models on the country as well as the regional level. We will use this as a \textbf{showcase} of the models capabilities, and comment on the estimated parameters, especially $P$, which accounts for cross-regional infections.
Second, we show that our model is able to give reasonable \textbf{one-week ahead forecasts} of \gls{c19} cases by comparing its predictive performance to that of real-time forecasts created in the ECDC's ForecastHub from April 2021 to January 2022.

\paragraph{Showcase}
Similar to the last section, we start out with a motivational example on the capabilities of our model. For this, we use the local outbreak highlighted in \Cref{fig:cases_germany}, which occurred in the middle of June 2020 \citep{Gunther2020SARSCoV2}. We consider the time period from 25 April 2020\footnote{Recall that this weekly observation includes cases reported from 19 April 2020 to 25 April 2020.} until 20 June 2020 as observations, consider the observation for 27 June 2020 to be missing, and fit the \gls{ssm} to this data. Throughout this example, let us denote that date by $T$. \Cref{fig:regional_showcase_prediction} shows the number of reported cases used to fit this model, aggregated to the whole of Germany. The local outbreak accounts for a total of $1\,413$ cases in the county of Gütersloh between 17 June 2020 and 23 June 2020 --- see \citep[Figure 1]{Gunther2020SARSCoV2} --- which is noticeable in our figure by the large jump between 13 June 2020 ($2324$ cases) and 20 June 2020 ($3872$ cases).

The estimated auto-regressive parameter $\alpha$ is negative, which can be interpreted as follows: when a region's log-growth factor is above the country-wide average at time $t$, it tends to decrease below the average in the following week. Similarly, regions with below-average growth factors tend to increase back toward the mean. This negative autocorrelation is sensible for the low-incidence setting we consider here, where large outbreaks are typically localized and temporary, rather than sustained.

The standard deviation of the average growth rate, $\sigma_{\overline{\log \rho}}$, is small compared to that of the spatial effect, $\sigma_{S}$. This is visible from \Cref{fig:regional_showcase_rho.tex} as well, where we show the predicted median growth factors (not on the log scale) for the model with a blue dashed line ($\overline{\log \rho}_{t}$) and boxplots for the median growth rates for each county (summarized by blue boxplots). Here we can see that the variation over time at the county level is much larger than that at the country level, which is also evident when examining $\log \rho_{t,r}$ directly (figures not shown).

The estimated overdispersion parameter $\phi$ indicates overdispersion in the data: as explained in \Cref{ex:pois_negbinom}, the variance of a $\operatorname{NegBinom}$ distribution with mean $\mu$ and overdispersion parameter $\phi$ is $1 + \frac{\mu}{\phi}$ times larger than that of a Poisson distribution with the same mean. 

This overdispersion becomes practically relevant when $\mu \geq \phi$, as this corresponds to at least doubling the variance compared to a Poisson model. For the date of 20 June 2020, this threshold was exceeded by 32 out of 400 counties, indicating that overdispersion was relevant for the higher incidence counties during this period.

% comment on predictions and neg binom baselines
Having fitted the model, we can use it to obtain forecasts for the next observation, that on 27 June 2020, using the techniques detailed in \Cref{subsec:inference}. We show predictions (median, $50\%$ and $95\%$ prediction intervals) in \Cref{fig:regional_showcase_prediction}, indicated by the blue regions. As the predictions on the country level consist of those on the regional level, we additionally use the data on total inhabitants in a county, capping the number of predicted cases by the total inhabitants, that is we predict in region $r$ with samples
$$
    \min \left\{\log \rho^{i}_{T,r}I_{T-1,r}, \text{pop}_{r}\right\},
$$
where $i=1,\dots,N$, $T$ is the index for which we want to perform predictions and $\text{pop}_{r}$ is the total number of inhabitants in each region.

To assess the performance, we compare it to three simple baseline models. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item The \texttt{nbinom} model uses a $ \operatorname{NegBinom}$ distribution with mean $\mu = 6451.1$ set to the country wide growth factor from 13 June 2020 to 20 June 2020 ($\rho = 1.67$) times the total number of cases reported for Germany for 20 June 2020 ($I_{t,\cdot} = 3872$). The overdispersion parameter is estimated by fitting the model to past observations with maximum likelihood estimation, resulting in $\phi = 143.1$. The median and prediction intervals presented in the figure are given by the respective quantiles of this distribution.
    \item The \texttt{reg.nbinom} model uses the same strategy, but applied to observations on the county level, estimating an individual $\phi$ for each county. To take care of unrealisticly high growth factors, we cap all growth factors at $3$. Should the empirical growth factor be infinite or indeterminante due to the number of old cases being $0$, we set it to $1$.  The median and prediction intervals presented in the figure are obtained by simulating $10\,000$ samples from each county, independently, then taking empirical prediction intervals on the number of cases aggregated to the country level. We also apply the same capping strategy by the total number of inhabitants as for the \texttt{ssm} model.
    \item The \texttt{reg.nbinom no GL} model is the same as the \texttt{reg.nbinom} model, but with cases for the county of Gütersloh set to $0$. 
\end{itemize}

It is clear from \Cref{fig:regional_showcase_prediction} that the \texttt{ssm} approach outperforms the baseline models: it is the only model where the prediction interval contains, or is at least close to the true observation. There are multiple reasons for this behavior. First, the simple \texttt{nbinom} overpredicts the number of total cases because the number of reported cases from the local outbreak in Gütersloh county dominate the country-wide development of cases. While the regional level \texttt{reg.nbinom} model is able to account for this, the empirical growth factor for Gütersloh is $\hat \rho_{T, \text{Gütersloh}} = 11.7$ with $856$ cases reported for 20 June 2020, which results in $\approx 10\,000$ cases predicted for that county alone. Recalling that we have capped the growth factor at $3$, the \texttt{reg.nbinom}  model only predicts $\approx 2\,600$ cases, which is still noticeable in the predictions. 
The best of the three baseline models --- in terms of predictive performance for this single observation --- is the \texttt{reg.nbinom no GL} model, where we have removed Gütersloh from the prediction completely. However, it still overpredicts by a relevant margin which we attribute to the fact that the same effect --- albeit less pronounced --- may be at work at other counties, too: due to small numbers of reported cases there are many large outliers in the empirical growth factors, see also \Cref{fig:regional_showcase_rho.tex}.

Another advantage of the \gls{ssm} model are the regional and country level growth factors we obtain. In the bottom part of \Cref{fig:regional_showcase_rho.tex} we show estimates of these growth factors, together with their empirical counterparts, i.e. the growth factors used in the \texttt{nbinom} and \texttt{reg.nbinom} baseline models (without the cap at $\rho = 3$ introduced there), given by
$$
\rho_{t}^{\text{emp}} = \frac{\sum_{r = 1}^R I_{t, r}}{\sum_{r = 1}^{R} I_{t - 1, r}}.
$$
For the country level \gls{ssm} approach, we show two variants: the first consist of the average growth factor over all counties, 
$$
    \rho_{t}^{\text{mean}} = \frac{1}{R} \sum_{r = 1}^R \rho_{t,r},
$$
as well as an adjusted estimate based on weighing the regional $\rho_{t,r}$ with the number of current reported cases
$$
    \rho_{t}^{\text{adj}} = \frac{\sum_{r = 1}^R\rho_{t,r} I_{t,r}}{\sum_{r = 1}^R I_{t, r}},
$$
both of which we estimate by importance sampling. 

In \Cref{fig:regional_showcase_rho.tex}, the adjusted and empirical growth factors mostly agree, except for the last date where the local outbreak described in \citep{Gunther2020SARSCoV2} occurred, where both \gls{ssm} based approaches produce smaller estimates due to the regularization introduced by our model. The mean growth factor differs from the other two approaches: it tends to be larger during periods of lower incidence and smaller during the local outbreak. This difference arises because $\rho_{t}^{\text{mean}}$ measures the average growth factor across all regions, while $\rho_{t}^{\text{adj}}$ measures the country-wide growth. To illustrate, consider the observations from 30 May 2020. The mean growth factor is close to $1$, indicating that an \glqq{}average\grqq{} region will experience stagnating cases. However, the adjusted growth factor is noticeably smaller than $1$, indicating that cases will decline at the country level. These observations are not contradictory: regions with large growth factors may have smaller incidences. Thus, taking both indicators into consideration is worthwhile.

For the county level growth factors in the upper figure of \Cref{fig:regional_showcase_rho.tex}, we additionally observe less variation of growth factors across counties due to the regularization our model imposes.

To further demonstrate the regional capabilities of our model, we focus on Gütersloh, where the outbreak occurred, and its neighboring counties. In \Cref{fig:regional_showcase_guetersloh}, we show a map of the distribution of the exchange matrix entries $p_{\text{GL}, r}$ for neighboring regions $r$ to the region $\text{GL}$. Most individuals stay in Gütersloh, with some exchange to Bielefeld, Stadt and Warendorf. In the bottom of that figure, we show growth factors for these counties, both the empirical growth factor and the model-based $\rho_{t,r}$ with prediction intervals, as well as $\bar\rho$. The empirical and model-based growth factors mostly agree --- except for some smoothing, especially if incidences are small in that region ---, but their use for prediction is different. 


Recall that $T$ is the date for which we want to perform predictions, i.e.\ 27 June 2020. The model we have fitted produces a prediction for the new growth factors $\rho_{T, r}$, based on the model's state dynamics for $\overline{\log \rho_{t}}$ and $u_{t,r}$. As $\alpha$ is negative, $\rho_{T,r}$ is notably smaller than $\rho_{T - 1, r}$ for most of the regions considered here. An empirical approach would simply use the last observed value of $\rho^{\text{emp}}_{T-1,r} = \frac{I_{T-1,r}}{I_{T - 2, r}}$ to predict the future $\hat\rho^{\text{emp}}_{T, r}$.  Thus, our model should predict smaller incidences for counties with large $\rho$, such as Gütersloh, compared to the empirical approach; see below. Another exception to the agreement of the two growth factors is in the presence of outliers, either large or small growth factors, which our model smooths out due to the small area effect. Importantly, our model can also provide prediction intervals for the growth factors, which the empirical approach cannot.

Another advantage of the fitted model is, that we can also investigate the distribution of secondary cases across regions. In particular, we can address the question of how many predicted cases in a given region are attributable to its proximity to Gütersloh. However, we stress that this should not be interpreted as an appropriate reconstruction of the actual transmission dynamics, and as such, the results must be treated with care. Indeed, the outbreak occurred in a meat processing plant with worker accommodations nearby, so there is reason to believe that primary cases did not travel extensively between regions.

For the regions under consideration, we show $I_{T, r \to r'} = \rho_{T,r'} p_{r,r'}I_{T-1,r}$, the number of secondary cases in regions $r'$ whose primary case is in region $r$, in \Cref{fig:guetersloh_neighbors_cases}, together with $95\%$ prediction intervals (all values rounded to integers). We observe that there is some contribution of Gütersloh's cases to Bielefelds' cases, and fewer to Warendorfs' --- these are the regions with high $p_{\text{GL}, r'}$ values. For Warendorf, this contribution is negligible compared to that of cases originating within Warendorf itself. For Bielefeld, the contribution from Gütersloh is more practically relevant, as the model predicts cases of similar magnitude from both sources.  Beyond these two regions, there is not much exchange, as both the number of cases and the $p_{r,r'}$ values are generally low (see also \Cref{fig:P_map_GL}).

With this in-depth analysis of regional dynamics performed, we now compare the regional \gls{ssm} predictions to those from the regional negative binomial model presented earlier in \Cref{fig:regional_showcase_prediction}, where prediction intervals are based on quantiles of the fitted negative binomial distribution on the regional level. The results are shown in \Cref{fig:regional_predictions_GL}. For regions with substantial exchange with Gütersloh, such as Bielefeld and Warendorf, the \gls{ssm} prediction intervals are notably wider than those from the local negative binomial model. Although the prediction intervals for the \gls{ssm} based approach tend to be larger overall, this difference is most notable for these two regions. 
This widening reflects the regional coupling in the \gls{ssm}, which captures uncertainty propagating between regions due to case exchange modeled by $P$. In contrast, the negative binomial model's prediction intervals are comparably narrow and do not account for this inter-regional effects. Overall, the \gls{ssm} approach produces prediction intervals that are wider and achieve better coverage, including the true observations more consistently, at least for the regions considered.

To investigate whether this holds up on the global scale, we evaluate the predictions made by the two models by several forecasting performance metrics across the different counties, as shown in \Cref{tab:performance_regional_ssm_vs_nbinom}. We use three different metrics: 
\begin{itemize}
    \item the \acrlong{mae}, where we take the median of the predictive distribution as a point forecast and average the absolute error between this point forecast and the true observation over all $400$ counties,
    \item the \acrlong{wis}\citep{Bracher2021Evaluating}, a proper scoring rule which approximates the continuous ranked probability score. It takes into account the whole predictive distribution, i.e. the centered prediction intervals provided in the forecasts. Lower values of WIS are better, and WIS can be decomposed into components measuring over- and underprediction as well as sharpness (length of predictive intervals). Finally, we also report
    \item the empirical coverage of $50\%$ and $95\%$ prediction intervals. 
\end{itemize}
Additionally, we report the decomposition of the \gls{wis} into its four components. 

The \gls{ssm} approach outperforms the regional negative binomial baseline in nearly all metrics. The only exceptions are the sharpness component of the \gls{wis}, as the negative binomial model produces smaller prediction intervals, and the coverage of $50\%$ prediction intervals, where the difference is negligible, as both models produce well calibrated $50\%$ prediction intervals. Overall, the joint \gls{ssm} appears to produce much more sensible forecasts than considering each county in isolation. In particular, the tails of the \gls{ssm} prediction intervals are better calibrated: the $95\%$ coverage is much closer to nominal, whereas for the negative binomial approach, tails of the predictive distribution are too light. We note that a Poisson baseline would likely perform even worse, as it cannot account for overdispersion as the negative binomial distribution can. An additional advantage of modeling all regions together is that we do not have to assume that regions evolve independently when computing aggregate forecasts, nor do we need to introduce artificial coupling mechanisms.

In summary, for the challenging prediction task in this showcase scenario, the \gls{ssm} severely outperforms the simple negative binomial baseline: on the country level (see \Cref{fig:regional_showcase_prediction}), on the individual region level, where we would expect prediction to be difficult (Gütersloh and its neighbors, see \Cref{fig:regional_predictions_GL}), and across all regions (see \Cref{tab:performance_regional_ssm_vs_nbinom}). One caveat is that by focusing on this singular predictive task, we might be fortunate that our model estimated a negative $\alpha$, as negative autocorrelation is beneficial in this setting --- it drives the predicted growth factor for Gütersloh downward after the outbreak. However, we will investigate predictive performance in varying scenarios below, which should address this concern.

\paragraph{One-week ahead forecasts}

In the last section we have seen that our regional \gls{ssm} provides accurate forecasts in a low incidence setting. We now want to investigate if that holds true in higher incidence settings.
To that end, we will compare the predictions made by our model to that of some models that participated in the \acrlong{gfch} \citep{Bracher2021Preregistered,Bracher2022National}, a collaborative forecasting challenge for \gls{c19} cases and deaths.
From 10 October 2020, the \gls{gfch} gathered weekly real-time forecasts for cases and deaths in Germany and Poland on the national and sub-national level. The participating teams were required to submit their forecast in a standardized format, providing selected quantiles from their predictive distributions. To evaluate the predictive performance of our model, we compare it to three models submitted to the Hub:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The \texttt{baseline} model takes the number of reported cases for this week as next weeks median, and adds uncertainty based on the past behavior of the epidemic.
    \item The \texttt{ensemble} model produces forecasts by averaging all submission to the \acrlong{gfch}, i.e. for each predictive quantile of one-week ahead cases, its predictive quantile is the average of all submissions for the same day, target and quantile, see \citep{Sherratt2023Predictive} for details.
    \item The \texttt{itww} model makes predictions based on a county-level reproduction number model, with forecasts based on repeated simulations from the renewal equation \eqref{eq:renewal-model}. See \citep{Heyder2022Regional} for details.
\end{itemize}

The \gls{efch} \citep{Sherratt2023Predictive} also gathered similar predictions, but we chose to focus on models submitted to the \gls{gfch} instead for two reasons. 
First, the \gls{efch} uses JHU \gls{c19} data \citep{Dong2020} as ground truth, rather than \gls{rki} data. The \gls{gfch} uses a dataset curated by the \gls{ecdc}, which is closer to \gls{rki} data as it is based on \gls{tessy} reports from the \gls{rki}. Second, as the \gls{gfch} explicitly gathered forecasts for Germany and Poland, we expect models submitted to it to produce more accurate forecasts than those submitted to the \gls{efch}. 

% switch KIT -> ECDC
For the \texttt{ensemble} model we use a combination of the \texttt{KITCOVIDhub-median-baseline} model, submitted to the \gls{gfch} and the \texttt{EuroCOVIDhub-ensemble} model, submitted to the \gls{efch}: starting with 10 April 2021, the \texttt{KITCOVIDhub-median-baseline} only aggregates subnational forecasts, not national ones. Instead the \texttt{EuroCOVIDhub-ensemble} is used in the \gls{gfch} from that date on, and so we do the same. The switch is indicated in \Cref{fig:regional_forecasts_comparison.tex} by a vertical line. 

To obtain the predictions for the \texttt{ssm} model, we aim at replicating the setup of the ForecastHubs as closely as possible. While the Hubs were active, forecasts could be submitted each Monday to predict the number of newly reported cases between the last Saturday and the next. For each forecast --- e.g. the first made for 17 October 2020 --- we use as observations the number of weekly reported cases that were available the Monday before --- e.g. on 12 October 2020 --- as reported by the \gls{rki} on that day. Of those, we use the last 9 weeks of weekly reported cases as observations and mark the last observation, that for the date to be forecasted, as missing. 

To fit the model, we use an abbreviated fitting and prediction procedure: we use only the initial guess of the parameter, $\hat\theta_{0}$ (\Cref{alg:mle}) which is obtained by at most $10$ iterations of a gradient descent procedure. Using this parameter, we obtain the \gls{la} (\Cref{alg:la}) of the posterior distribution and produce forecasts of the last, missing observation by using $1000$ samples from the \gls{la}, using the approach detailed in \Cref{subsec:inference}. The whole procedure can run in approximately 10 minutes for a single forecast on a consumer grade Macbook Air M3 with 16GB of RAM. 

To evaluate the quality of the forecasts, we use the final value the \gls{rki} reported, i.e. the dataset published on 5 May 2023, see \Cref{subsec:incidence_and_death_data}.

\subsection{Discussion}

The \gls{ssm} we have introduced in this section allows making sensible reconstructions, interpretation of parameters and produces well performing forecasts for both low and high incidence scenarios. By including an exchange of cases between different regions, our regional model is able to handle scenarios of low incidences gracefully. The main strength of our novel model is its balance between model complexity necessary to account for the regional level effects and interpretability, while still retaining noteworthy predictive performance. By focusing on weekly aggregation of cases, we are able to ignore weekday effects, which is well suited for practical applications, such as the \gls{gfch}. The price we have to pay for this is the loss of daily effects, such as the daily growth factor and reporting delays we used in \Cref{sec:model_reporting_delay}. 

The regional structure we have introduced allows detailed analyses on the regional level, as we have seen for the substantive analysis of the Gütersloh outbreak, as well as the state level forecasts.
Let us reiterate that our forecasting setup aims at replicating the available information during the real-time forecasts performed for the \gls{gfch} as closely as possible, to make the evaluation of the predictive performance valid. Indeed, our model even uses less information than is available, as the weekly aggregation ignores all cases reported on Sunday and Monday. 

Our model also comes with some disadvantages: the dimension of $m=401$ states is quite high, which makes fitting the model more costly than for the models presented in \Cref{sec:model_reporting_delay,sec:nowcasting_hospitalizations}, in fact so costly that we restrict ourselves to only $10$ time points. Estimating the MLE $\hat\theta$ with $N=10\,000$ samples for the showcase model takes 3 hours on a consumer grade Macbook Air M3, while obtaining the initial estimate of $\theta$, $\hat\theta_{0}$ is faster, taking only around $5$ minutes on the same computer. Let us note that for predictive performance in the one-week ahead forecasts, we have found no practical difference between the two fitted models, so we opted for the cheaper $\hat\theta_{0}$. 

Furthermore, we model the exchange between regions, given by $P$, as static over the whole period which is likely unrealistic for longer periods --- we have investigated the estimated $\hat C$ and $\hat{\bar q}$ in the one-week ahead forecast setting, and found substantial change (figures not shown). While it is theoretically possible to have $P$ vary over time (e.g. by including different $C$ and $\bar q$ for each point in time), it will make fitting the model much more involved. 

Additionally, let us reiterate that the inclusion of the commuter data comes with several challenges: the data are based on official records, which may be far off the real exchange between regions that occurs, e.g. due to home office policies or changes in mandatory social distancing in the periods considered. 

Finally, presenting a fair comparison between retrospective forecasts, such as the ones presented here, and the preregistered ones provided in the \gls{gfch} is challenging. While we have aimed at reconstructing the setting of these Forecast Hubs as closely as possible, it is important to note that these are not fully equivalent to real-time, preregistered forecasts. Retrospective analyses may inadvertently benefit from knowledge of future events or data revisions, potentially leading to optimistic estimates of predictive performance.

Our approach differs from existing literature in several key aspects. First, while many studies focus on either network-based or spatio-temporal models, we integrate elements from both paradigms: we use network-based effects (commuter data) to inform the exchange of cases between regions, while also allowing growth rates to evolve over time. This allows us to capture the complex interplay between regional dynamics and spatial dependencies more effectively. 

A further advantage of our model is that it provides interpretable results, which will be useful in informing public health decision-making, as we may interpret the growth factors $\rho_{t}$ and $\rho_{t,r}$ (with the caveats provided in the discussion surrounding \Cref{fig:regional_showcase_rho.tex}) epidemiologically. Additionally, the hyperparameters $C$ and $\bar q$ also have interpretations in terms of population structure and mobility, although this interpretation has to be more careful. 

In conclusion, our model addresses several gaps identified in our short literature review at the start of this section, by providing a flexible, yet interpretable framework for modeling the spatio-temporal spread of an infectious disease such as \gls{c19}. Compared to network-based \citep{Armbruster2024Networkbased} and spatio-temporal approaches \citep{Kline2022Bayesian}, our method achieves a balance between complexity, interpretability and predictive performance. While the aforementioned limitations remain, these allow for promising avenues for future research, by including dynamic mobility modeling and real-time forecast evaluation, e.g. in the \gls{ecdc}'s RespiCast Hub \citep{ECDCRespiCast}. Overall, we believe this model to provide a valuable contribution to the toolkit of epidemic modeling at the regional level, with potential application beyond the current context.
